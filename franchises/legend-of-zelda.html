📖 Franchise History
1986 — The Origin
Shigeru Miyamoto & Takashi Tezuka Create Hyrule
Miyamoto conceived Zelda as the opposite of Mario — exploration over linear progression, puzzles over reaction. Inspired by his childhood explorations of the countryside near Kyoto, the original game let players roam freely. The name "Zelda" came from Zelda Fitzgerald. The Famicom Disk System original (1986) and NES cartridge (1987) established the gold cartridge, the Master Sword, and the Triforce as icons. Koji Kondo composed the overworld theme that remains one of gaming's most recognizable pieces of music.
1987–1993 — Experimentation
Zelda II, ALttP, and Finding the Formula
Zelda II (1987) was a radical departure — side-scrolling RPG combat that confused players expecting the original's top-down format. It remains the outlier. A Link to the Past (1991) returned to top-down gameplay but vastly expanded the formula with the Light/Dark World, a proper narrative, and the iconic Master Sword quest. ALttP defined "Zelda" as a concept for a generation. Link's Awakening (1993) brought the formula to Game Boy with surprising emotional depth — no Triforce, no Ganon, just a dream.
1998–2000 — The N64 Revolution
Ocarina of Time and Majora's Mask Redefine the Medium
Ocarina of Time (1998) achieved what seemed impossible — a perfect translation of the Zelda formula into 3D. Z-targeting, context-sensitive controls, and a sweeping orchestral score (MIDI) made it a landmark. It received perfect scores from nearly every major publication and held the title of highest-rated game on Metacritic for over a decade. Majora's Mask (2000) was developed in 1.5 years with the same engine — darker, stranger, built around a three-day time loop and masks that absorbed the souls of the dead. It found its audience slowly and is now considered equally essential.
2002–2006 — Style and Controversy
Wind Waker's Cel Shading and Twilight Princess's Redemption
The Wind Waker (2002) was unveiled at Space World 2000 as a realistic tech demo, then re-revealed as a cel-shaded cartoon — sparking the "Celda" backlash. It shipped late and undersold. Time has been kind: WW is now widely considered one of the most beautiful games ever made. Twilight Princess (2006) was developed as a direct response to WW's reception — dark, grounded, photorealistic. Launched on both Wii and GC, it gave the "realistic Zelda" crowd what they wanted while introducing Midna, the franchise's most beloved partner character.
2011–2016 — The HD Gap and Handheld Excellence
Skyward Sword Divides, 3DS Delivers
Skyward Sword (2011) was designed around Wii MotionPlus — every puzzle and combat encounter tuned to 1:1 sword tracking. The motion controls divided critics and players sharply. The 3DS era quietly produced some of the best Zelda games: Ocarina of Time 3D (2011) and Majora's Mask 3D (2015) were near-perfect remakes, while A Link Between Worlds (2013) was an original masterpiece that proved top-down Zelda still had room to innovate. The WiiU received Wind Waker HD (2013) and Twilight Princess HD (2016) but never its promised original Zelda title.
2017–Present — Open World Revolution
Breath of the Wild, Tears of the Kingdom, and a Live-Action Film
Breath of the Wild (2017) launched alongside the Nintendo Switch and dismantled 30 years of Zelda conventions. No prescribed dungeon order. Climb any surface. Cook ingredients into meals. Approach every encounter creatively. It won Game of the Year at every major ceremony and redefined open-world design industry-wide. Tears of the Kingdom (2023) expanded on BotW's foundation with Ultrahand construction mechanics, a vast underground Depths layer, and sky islands — receiving 10/10 scores and near-universal acclaim. In 2023, Nintendo and Sony Pictures announced a live-action theatrical film directed by Wes Ball (The Maze Runner), with Shigeru Miyamoto producing — the first major live-action Nintendo film since the 1993 Super Mario Bros. disaster.
