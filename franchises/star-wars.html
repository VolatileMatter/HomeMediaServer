Star Wars

// Films · TV · Games · Mobile · EmulatorJS · Jellyfin //

George Lucas's space opera spanning 1977 to the present. The franchise encompasses 11 theatrical films, a dozen live-action and animated series, and over 100 games across every platform from Atari to modern consoles. The Library tab covers every piece of media ever released — including defunct, cancelled, and mobile-only entries. Canon (Disney continuity) vs Legends (pre-2014 Expanded Universe) is marked throughout.

Canon vs Legends at a glance — In 2014, Disney declared all pre-acquisition Expanded Universe content Legends (non-canon). The new Canon starts with The Clone Wars CGI series and everything released after. The films are all canon. KOTOR, TIE Fighter, Shadows of the Empire — all Legends, but still excellent games worth playing.

🛒 Star Wars Merchandise History

Star Wars invented modern franchise merchandising. Kenner's 1977 toy deal, struck when nobody thought a space film would succeed, became the template every franchise since has followed. The license has generated more merchandise revenue than the films themselves.

1977–1985 — The Kenner Era

Kenner's 3¾" action figure line became the gold standard for toy licensing. Famously, demand outstripped supply so severely at Christmas 1977 that Kenner shipped empty "Early Bird" vouchers that parents gave as gifts, redeemable for figures months later. The line eventually encompassed hundreds of figures, vehicles, and playsets.

1985–1994 — The Dark Times

After Return of the Jedi, Kenner wound down the line. Star Wars merchandise nearly disappeared from shelves for a decade. Only the Expanded Universe novels (Heir to the Empire, 1991) kept the property alive culturally during this dormant period.

1995–1999 — The Hasbro Revival

Hasbro (which acquired Kenner) relaunched the 3¾" line in 1995 in anticipation of the Special Edition theatrical releases. The Power of the Force line was enormously successful — overly muscled compared to originals, but beloved by collectors. The prequel era (The Phantom Menace, 1999) brought a new wave of products including pod racer toys, LEGO Star Wars, and video game tie-ins.

2012–Present — The Disney Era

Disney's $4 billion acquisition of Lucasfilm in 2012 brought a global merchandising operation. Black Series (6" collector figures, 2013–present) and The Vintage Collection (3¾" premium line) are current collector standards. LEGO Star Wars remains one of LEGO's most popular themes. The Mandalorian launched a secondary merchandise wave centered on Grogu ("Baby Yoda") in 2019.

📖 Franchise History
📅 1971–1977 — Origins
George Lucas Bets His Career on a Space Fairy Tale
After American Graffiti's success gave Lucas negotiating leverage, he secured rights to retain Star Wars sequel and merchandise rights from 20th Century Fox in exchange for a lower directorial fee — a deal Fox accepted because nobody expected a space opera to be profitable. The production was notoriously difficult: on-set breakdowns, overrunning budgets, and a film that almost every studio insider expected to fail. Star Wars opened May 25, 1977, and earned $461 million worldwide on a $11 million budget. Kenner's toy deal, signed in the same era of low expectations, made Lucas a billionaire independently of the films.
📅 1977–1986 — The Lucas Era
The Original Trilogy and the Birth of Modern Merchandising
The Empire Strikes Back (1980) and Return of the Jedi (1983) completed the trilogy while Lucasfilm's ILM division revolutionized visual effects for every film that followed. The franchise established that a movie could be a platform for toys, books, comics, and games — a model every major studio now follows. After RotJ, Lucas stepped back from active filmmaking, producing the Ewok TV movies and animated series before entering a decade of franchise dormancy.
📅 1991–1998 — The Expanded Universe Revival
Timothy Zahn and the Legends Foundation
Timothy Zahn's Heir to the Empire (1991) revived mainstream Star Wars interest with a New York Times bestselling continuation of the saga. The Expanded Universe of novels, comics (Dark Horse), and games (TIE Fighter, Dark Forces, KOTOR) built a vast secondary canon that thousands of fans considered the "real" sequel era. This period also saw the 1995 Hasbro toy relaunch and the 1997 Special Edition theatrical re-releases building anticipation for the prequel trilogy.
📅 1999–2005 — The Prequel Era
The Most Divisive Films in Blockbuster History
The Phantom Menace (1999) arrived with unprecedented hype and generated passionate debate about Jar Jar Binks, midichlorians, and young Anakin's acting. Attack of the Clones (2002) and Revenge of the Sith (2005) received progressively warmer receptions, with RotS widely considered the strongest entry. Genndy Tartakovsky's 2003 Clone Wars micro-series and the 2008 CGI Clone Wars TV show significantly deepened the era's narrative. LucasArts' game output during this period (KOTOR, Republic Commando, Battlefront, Jedi Outcast) represents the franchise's gaming peak.
📅 2012 — The Disney Acquisition
$4.05 Billion and a Legends Stamp
Disney acquired Lucasfilm for $4.05 billion in October 2012. Within months, LucasArts was shuttered (cancelling Star Wars 1313 and First Assault), and the Lucasfilm Story Group declared the entire pre-acquisition Expanded Universe non-canon under the "Legends" label. A new Story Group would manage a unified, coherent canon going forward. Fan reaction ranged from grief (for decades of beloved novels) to cautious optimism about new films.
📅 2015–2019 — The Sequel Trilogy
The Galaxy Divided Again
The Force Awakens (2015) was a commercial juggernaut but divided fans over its structural similarities to A New Hope. Rogue One (2016) was broadly celebrated as the franchise's best film since the OT. The Last Jedi (2017) became one of cinema's most contested films — critically acclaimed but fandom-divisive. Solo (2018) underperformed at the box office, signalling franchise fatigue. The Rise of Skywalker (2019) attempted course correction and divided audiences further. The Disney trilogy era ended without consensus.
📅 2019–Present — The Disney+ Era
Grogu, Andor, and the Streaming Pivot
The Mandalorian (2019) and the cultural phenomenon of Grogu revitalized mainstream enthusiasm. Andor (2022) received near-universal critical acclaim as the franchise's finest writing. The streaming model enabled more diverse storytelling than theatrical releases — though also produced mixed-reception shows (Boba Fett, Obi-Wan, The Acolyte). In games, Respawn's Jedi series (Fallen Order, Survivor) and Massive's Outlaws represent a new era of canon-integrated single-player experiences. As of 2026, several theatrical films and additional series are in development, with the franchise's future focused on recovering the post-sequel era identity.

🗺️ Canon Map
What's canon? What's Legends?

In April 2014, the Lucasfilm Story Group announced that all media prior to the Disney acquisition (except the six theatrical films and The Clone Wars CGI series) would be rebranded as "Legends" — a parallel continuity that "may not reflect the most current canon." Everything produced from 2014 onward under Disney's Story Group is Canon.
