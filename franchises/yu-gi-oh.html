📺 Yu-Gi-Oh! TV & Anime
Nine Series, Three Eras

Yu-Gi-Oh! has produced nine anime series across three eras. The original trilogy (Duel Monsters / GX / 5D's) shares a rough connected continuity. The modern era (Zexal, Arc-V, VRAINS) exists as standalone series. The Rush Duel era (Sevens / Go Rush!!) is its own independent continuity. Each series introduces a new summoning mechanic alongside the new protagonist.

🛒 Yu-Gi-Oh! Merchandise
Physical TCG & Collectibles

The Yu-Gi-Oh! TRADING CARD GAME is the #1 TCG in the world by total cards sold (35+ billion). Physical cards are published by Konami. Structure Decks are beginner-friendly pre-built decks. Booster Packs expand the collection. Tin sets and special editions include hard-to-find cards. For digital card collecting without physical storage, Master Duel is the recommended alternative.

📖 Franchise History
📅 1996–1998 — Origin
From Manga to Monster
Kazuki Takahashi began serializing Yu-Gi-Oh! in Weekly Shonen Jump in 1996. The early manga was a dark gaming anthology — Yugi Muto could challenge bullies to Shadow Games with lethal stakes. The card game "Duel Monsters" was one of many mini-games in the manga, but Toei Animation adapted the early chapters into a 27-episode anime in 1998 that largely ignored it. Reader response to the Duel Monsters card segments was overwhelmingly positive, leading Konami and Bandai to produce the first physical card game — and the first video game (GB, 1998) — before the manga even centered on it.
📅 1999–2004 — Global Explosion
Duel Monsters Conquers the World
Studio Gallop's Yu-Gi-Oh! Duel Monsters (2000) ran for 224 episodes and made the franchise a global phenomenon. The physical TCG launched internationally through Upper Deck (North America) and expanded across dozens of countries. The video game line exploded across Game Boy, GBC, GBA, PS1, and PS2 — culminating in iconic handheld titles like The Eternal Duelist Soul and Forbidden Memories. The manga concluded in 2004, the same year GX launched to hand off the torch.
📅 2004–2011 — The Spinoff Era
GX, 5D's, and the PSP/DS Golden Age
GX (2004–2008) and 5D's (2008–2011) continued the franchise through two critically respected series. GX remains divisive but its final arc is some of the darkest storytelling in the franchise; 5D's is broadly considered the best-plotted spin-off. The PSP Tag Force series (TF1–TF6) defined the era for handheld fans — Tag Force 5 alone contains over 4,500 cards and 300+ hours. The DS World Championship series ran parallel, providing accessible annual card sim updates.
📅 2011–2019 — Diversification
Zexal, Arc-V, VRAINS — and the Rise of Mobile
Zexal introduced Xyz Summoning and ran to 146 episodes. Arc-V (2014) introduced Pendulum Summoning and a multiverse-spanning story that directly references all prior series. VRAINS (2017) brought a cyberpunk aesthetic and Link Summoning. On the games side, Konami shifted strategy: Duel Links (2016) was the mobile breakthrough — Speed Duels with short sessions proved enormously successful. Legacy of the Duelist (2015, expanded as Link Evolution 2019) brought narrative retrospectives to console. The Tag Force and World Championship handheld lines ended; console and mobile became the new focus.
📅 2020–2022 — Tragedy and the Rush Duel Era
Kazuki Takahashi's Death and the New Generation
Yu-Gi-Oh! Sevens (2020) launched a new era with Rush Duels — a simplified format aimed at younger audiences with a new generation of characters. In July 2022, franchise creator Kazuki Takahashi passed away unexpectedly at age 60. His final major work had been The Dark Side of Dimensions (2016), which he personally oversaw. Master Duel launched in January 2022 to widespread acclaim as the definitive digital TCG — 10,000+ cards, competitive ranked play, and full official rules. Cross Duel (a 4-player mobile experiment) launched and was shuttered in the same year, lasting exactly 12 months.
📅 2022–Present
Go Rush!!, Early Days Collection, and the 25th Anniversary
Go Rush!! (2022) continues as the current anime series in Japan. Master Duel remains the dominant competitive digital experience with consistent updates. Konami celebrated the franchise's 25th anniversary in 2023–2025 with events, commemorative products, and the Early Days Collection (February 2025) — a Digital Eclipse-developed compilation of 14 classic Game Boy/GBA games, including five released outside Japan for the first time. The physical TCG continues to expand with new sets, summoning mechanics, and yearly world championships. The franchise shows no signs of slowing; it remains one of Konami's most valuable IP.

🗺️ Canon Map
What's canon, what's not?

The franchise has three rough continuity blocs. The original trilogy (Duel Monsters → GX → 5D's) shares a connected future-timeline: GX is set ~10 years after DM, 5D's is set centuries further in the future. Bonds Beyond Time explicitly crossovers all three. Zexal, Arc-V, and VRAINS are standalone continuities with no direct connection to the trilogy (though Arc-V pays heavy homage). Sevens and Go Rush!! form their own Rush Duel continuity.

The Toei Season 0 anime is based on the early manga chapters but diverges significantly from both the manga and Gallop anime — it's generally considered a standalone adaptation. The Pyramid of Light movie is disputed canon. The Dark Side of Dimensions was made with Takahashi's direct involvement and is considered fully canonical to the manga ending. Video games are generally non-canon unless they adapt anime story arcs directly.
