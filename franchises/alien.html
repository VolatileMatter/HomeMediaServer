📖 Franchise History
1974–1978
Giger & O'Bannon — Origins
Dan O'Bannon wrote a script called "Memory" (later retitled "Alien") after his experience on the aborted Jodorowsky Dune project. Simultaneously, Swiss surrealist H.R. Giger was painting the Necronomicon series — biomechanical landscapes merging human anatomy with industrial machinery. Producer Gordon Carroll brought the two together when the script landed at Fox. Ridley Scott was hired as director in 1977.
1979
Alien — The Original Film
Alien opened May 25, 1979. Giger designed the Xenomorph, the Space Jockey, and the derelict spacecraft — winning the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects. The film starred Sigourney Weaver as Ellen Ripley, establishing one of cinema's first major female action protagonists. It grossed $104 million on an $11 million budget. Fox immediately greenlit a sequel but Ridley Scott declined to return.
1982–1987
Early Games & Merchandise
Fox Video Games released the Atari 2600 Alien in 1982 — a Pac-Man style maze game. Kenner produced the infamous 18-inch Alien action figure in 1979, quickly pulled from shelves due to parent complaints (it was designed for adults). CRL Group released text adventures on home computers from 1984 onward. Electric Dreams adapted the Aliens film for Amiga and DOS in 1987.
1986
Aliens — Cameron's Military Expansion
James Cameron rewrote O'Bannon's world as a military science fiction action epic. The Colonial Marines, the M41A Pulse Rifle, the APC, and the Dropship all debuted here. Aliens grossed $131 million and earned Weaver an Academy Award nomination for Best Actress — a first for an action/horror role. The film defined the visual language of the franchise's military side.
1988–1996
Dark Horse Comics & EU Expansion
Dark Horse Comics launched the Aliens comic line in 1988, creating an entire expanded universe before Alien 3 arrived. Their original continuity — where Hicks and Newt survived and went on to fight Alien infestations on Earth — was abruptly contradicted by the third film. Dark Horse retconned all their characters to avoid the conflict. The AvP crossover debuted in 1989 and became one of comics' most successful franchise mashups.
1992–1997
Alien 3, Resurrection & Fox's Troubled Years
Alien 3's production went through five directors before Fincher was hired. The studio imposed changes that Fincher publicly disowned. The film opened to mixed reviews. Alien Resurrection (1997) under Jeunet fared similarly. Joss Whedon's script was significantly altered during production. Fox's aggressive interference became a cautionary tale in franchise management. The line went dormant for theatrical releases until 2004.
1994–2001
Rebellion's AvP Games — The PC Golden Era
Rebellion Developments produced the Atari Jaguar AvP in 1994 — one of the first FPS games with genuine horror atmosphere. Their 1999 PC follow-up, published by Fox Interactive, is widely considered the best Alien game ever made. Three complete campaigns (Marine, Alien, Predator) each with distinct gameplay. Monolith Productions' AvP2 (2001) deepened the narrative. Both games remain available through GOG.
2004–2013
AvP Films, Colonial Marines & the Nadir
Paul W.S. Anderson's Alien vs Predator (2004) and the Strauses' Requiem (2007) were commercially successful but critically reviled. Gearbox's Aliens: Colonial Marines (2013) became notorious for misrepresented pre-release footage — the shipped game looked nothing like the trailers. A lawsuit was settled. Community modder "TemplarGFX" later discovered and fixed a single-character typo in the game's AI code that made the entire game significantly more competent. The "intentionally bad" AI had been broken by a missing 'a' in a config file for years.
2014
Alien: Isolation — The Rehabilitation
Creative Assembly's Alien: Isolation restored the franchise's gaming reputation in a single release. The game drew directly from Giger's original film designs rather than the Cameron military aesthetic, and created one of gaming's most sophisticated AI opponents. The single Xenomorph enemy uses two independent AI systems that both observe the player without direct communication — one hunts by stimuli, one hunts by prediction. The game won multiple BAFTA awards and is considered among the finest survival horror games ever made.
2012–2017
The Prequel Trilogy Attempt
Ridley Scott returned to the franchise with Prometheus (2012), intended as the first of a trilogy explaining the Space Jockey. The film was divisive — praised for its visuals and David the android, criticized for its script logic. Alien: Covenant (2017) attempted to course-correct while still advancing the prequel storyline. The third prequel film was never made as Fox's acquisition by Disney shuffled franchise priorities.
2019–Present
Disney Era & Renaissance
Disney's acquisition of Fox brought the Alien IP under new management. Noah Hawley (Fargo, Legion) was commissioned to develop an FX television series — Alien: Earth — set on present-day Earth before the original film. It premiered in 2024 to exceptional reviews and strong viewership. Simultaneously, Fede Álvarez (Evil Dead remake) directed Alien: Romulus (2024), a theatrical film set between Alien and Aliens that received the franchise's best critical reception since the original Cameron sequel. Tindalos Interactive's Aliens: Dark Descent (2023) restored gaming's confidence in the property. The franchise is currently in its strongest position since 1986.
2014 (May 12)
H.R. Giger — In Memoriam
Hans Ruedi Giger died on May 12, 2014, from injuries sustained in a fall. He was 74. His biomechanical art, expressed through paintings, sculpture, and the Alien design, permanently altered the visual vocabulary of science fiction and horror. The Giger Museum in Gruyères, Switzerland, remains open. His Academy Award for Alien (1980) was the only Oscar ever awarded for creature design at the time.
