📖 Franchise History
1993–1996
Origins at Core Design
Tomb Raider was conceived at Core Design in Derby, UK, by a small team led by Toby Gard. The original protagonist was a male archaeologist, then briefly a woman named Laura Cruz, before being anglicized to Lara Croft. Gard designed the character primarily as a reaction against the hypersexualized depictions common in gaming at the time — she was intended to be competent and self-sufficient, not titillating. The game launched in October 1996 for Sega Saturn and PlayStation, followed by PC. It sold 2.5 million units by 1997 and made Lara Croft an immediate global icon.
1997–2003
The Annual Release Treadmill & Core Design's Collapse
Publisher Eidos Interactive demanded annual Christmas releases, forcing Core Design to produce sequels at a punishing pace. Toby Gard left Core Design shortly after TR1 shipped, citing creative differences. TR2 through TR5 (Chronicles) released each year from 1997 to 2000 under intense crunch. By Angel of Darkness (2003) the team was burned out, the engine was unstable, and the game shipped in a broken state. Eidos pulled the franchise from Core Design and transferred it to Crystal Dynamics in California. Core Design was eventually sold to Rebellion in 2006 and closed as Rebellion Derby in 2010.
2001–2003
The Jolie Films
Two live-action films were produced: Lara Croft: Tomb Raider (2001) and The Cradle of Life (2003), both starring Angelina Jolie. Both were commercial successes despite critical panning. They kept Lara Croft in mainstream pop culture during the decline of the Core Design games. Jolie's casting remains one of the most recognized pieces of video game film casting history.
2006–2008
Crystal Dynamics' Legend Trilogy Reboot
Crystal Dynamics completely rebuilt the franchise with Tomb Raider: Legend (2006), introducing a new Lara with fluid controls, a Hollywood action tone, and a fresh story unconnected to the Core Design games. Legend, Anniversary, and Underworld formed a tight trilogy with a continuous narrative about Lara's mother and Thor's gauntlets. The trilogy was well-received and stabilized the franchise commercially, though it never reached the cultural saturation of the 1996–1998 era.
2009–2012
Square Enix Acquisition & Survivor Trilogy Development
Eidos Interactive was acquired by Square Enix in April 2009, bringing Tomb Raider into the Square Enix family. Crystal Dynamics began work on a complete franchise reboot — a gritty origin story for a younger, more vulnerable Lara, heavily inspired by Naughty Dog's Uncharted and survival horror aesthetics. Rhianna Pratchett was brought on as lead writer.
2013–2018
The Survivor Trilogy
Tomb Raider (2013) became the fastest-selling game in Square Enix history and the best-selling TR entry ever at 14.5+ million copies. Rise of the Tomb Raider (2016) was a timed Xbox exclusive before releasing on PS4 and PC. Shadow of the Tomb Raider (2018), developed by Eidos Montréal, concluded the trilogy in Peru. The trilogy significantly expanded Lara's character but also drew criticism for its Uncharted-derivative tone. Dark Horse Comics published canonical tie-in comics during this period.
2018–2022
Vikander Film, Embracer Acquisition, Rights Turbulence
The 2018 Alicia Vikander film was better received than the Jolie films and aligned with the Survivor trilogy aesthetic. A sequel entered and exited development twice before MGM lost the rights in 2022. Embracer Group acquired the Tomb Raider IP alongside Eidos Montréal and Crystal Dynamics from Square Enix in 2022 for approximately $300M — the largest gaming acquisition that year.
2022–Present
CDE Entertainment, Amazon Series & Upcoming Games
Embracer Group transferred the IP to its subsidiary CDE Entertainment. Crystal Dynamics announced two simultaneous UE5 games: Legacy of Atlantis (developed by Flying Wild Hog) and Catalyst (Crystal Dynamics, set in Northern India). Both use Alix Wilton Regan as Lara and treat the Survivor trilogy as canon origin. Amazon confirmed a live-action Prime Video series with Phoebe Waller-Bridge writing and Sophie Turner as Lara. Netflix's animated series concluded with Season 2 in December 2025. The franchise is now in its most active multi-platform state since the late 1990s.

🗺️ Canon Map
Three Continuities — Not Compatible

Tomb Raider has three hard continuity breaks. The three Laras share a name and archetype but are not the same character in the same timeline. CDE's "Unified Timeline" attempts to retroactively connect the Survivor trilogy as the origin of all Laras going forward.

🎲 Notable Facts & Trivia
  • Lara Croft was originally named Laura Cruz. The name was anglicized to Lara Croft after Toby Gard went through British phone books looking for a "posh-sounding" surname.
  • The Guinness Book of World Records recognized Lara Croft as the "Most Successful Human Videogame Heroine" in 2006, a title she holds nearly two decades later.
  • Toby Gard, Lara's creator, left Core Design immediately after TR1 shipped over disagreements about how Lara was being marketed. Eidos famously dressed promotional models as Lara for events — something Gard opposed.
  • The leaked 2006 build of the canceled 10th Anniversary PSP remake is preserved on the Internet Archive and is fully playable. It represents a complete alternate history where Core Design, not Crystal Dynamics, made the TR1 remake.
  • TT Games produced a LEGO Lara Croft test animation in the mid-2000s but judged the franchise not big enough for a standalone game. The pitch became an Indiana Jones crossover concept — which also never happened in that form.
  • The Tomb Raider franchise has sold over 100 million units across all games — placing it among the top-selling video game series of all time.
  • Embracer Group purchased Crystal Dynamics, Eidos Montréal, and the Tomb Raider IP from Square Enix for roughly $300M in 2022 — Square Enix had paid approximately £400M for Eidos in 2009, meaning they sold it at a substantial loss.
  • The Amazon Prime Video live-action series marks the first time a Tomb Raider screen adaptation has had a female writer as the primary creative lead (Phoebe Waller-Bridge).
