Bungie Era 2001–2010
Halo CE through Reach. Bungie's original vision. The Forerunners were seeded as human ancestors — humanity devolved and punished by the Mantle after the Flood war. Never made fully explicit in-game, but embedded in terminals and the original lore bible. Master Chief as symbolic heir to the Forerunner legacy. Ends with Reach (2010).
343 Era 2012–present
Halo 4 onward. 343 retconned Forerunners into a separate alien species with humanity as a distinct lineage. The Didact and Librarian are now fleshed-out Forerunner characters. The Greg Bear Forerunner Saga novels (Cryptum, Primordium, Silentium) formalized the retcon. ⚡ Retcon marks entries that conflict with Bungie's original framing.

⚡ The Forerunner Retcon Explained
Bungie's Halo lore — in Halo 3 terminals, the original lore bible, and staff interviews — seeded the idea that ancient humans were the Forerunners: humanity devolved and punished by the Mantle after being consumed by the Flood threat. The Covenant worshipped Forerunner relics because they were, unknowingly, worshipping humanity's ancestors.

Greg Bear's Forerunner Saga, written in consultation with 343, instead establishes Forerunners as a distinct alien species. Humans exist in the same universe but are a different lineage — the Librarian "indexed" them separately. Neither version is wrong per se — the retcon just replaced one mythology with another. If you prefer Bungie's vision, treat Cryptum/Primordium/Silentium as an alternate branch and stop your reading at the 2010 line.

A brief history of the Halo merchandise program.
📅 Early Licensed Era (2001–2009)

The first wave of Halo merch was driven by Joyride Studios, who produced CE- and Halo 2-era action figures. Quality was respectable for the era and the line has nostalgic collector value. Microsoft also licensed art books through Prima Games. The market was primarily games retail adjacent — sold at GameStop and EB Games alongside the actual games.

📅 McFarlane Mass-Market Era (2010s)

McFarlane Toys became the dominant figure licensee from the Halo 3 era onward, producing consistent 5–6" figure lines for every major release through Halo 5. These are the most widely available Halo figures and represent excellent value. Dark Horse Comics picked up the art book license and produced significantly higher-quality volumes than the earlier Prima releases.

📅 Premium Collector Era (2015–present)

First 4 Figures entered the Halo space with premium 1/4-scale statues targeting adult collectors. Their CE Master Chief, ODST, and Arbiter pieces are the grails of the Halo statue market. Spartan Games produced a well-regarded but now out-of-print miniatures line (Fleet Battles, Ground Command). Jazwares replaced McFarlane as the current mass-market figure licensee for the Halo Infinite era.

Grail Items & Hard-to-Find Pieces

First 4 Figures limited editions (CE Chief, ODST, Arbiter) command premiums on the secondary market but remain fairly stable. Spartan Games Fleet Battles is out of print and sought after by miniature gamers — check eBay for incomplete starter sets if you want to play rather than collect. Joyride Studios early figures in box are increasingly rare and collectible.

📖 A History of Halo
Overview

Halo began as a real-time strategy game for Mac before Microsoft acquired Bungie in 2000 and pivoted it to a first-person shooter launch title for the original Xbox. Halo: Combat Evolved (2001) didn't just sell a console — it redefined what first-person shooters could be on consoles, establishing the two-weapon system, regenerating shields, and vehicle integration that became genre conventions. The franchise helped make Xbox Live viable, sold over 80 million units across all entries, and spawned one of the most robust extended-universe novel programs in gaming.

📅 1999–2001 — The Origin
From Mac RTS to Xbox FPS Launch Title
Bungie announced Halo at Macworld 1999 as a third-person real-time strategy game for Mac and Windows. Microsoft acquired Bungie in 2000, cancelled the Mac version, and pivoted the game into an Xbox-exclusive first-person shooter. CE shipped as an Xbox launch title in November 2001 and sold over five million copies. It established the tone, mechanics, and mystery of the Forerunner universe that would define the franchise for a decade.
📅 2004–2007 — The Golden Age
Halo 2, Xbox Live, and the Cultural Peak
Halo 2 (2004) launched Xbox Live as a viable online gaming platform. Its online multiplayer was transformative — hundreds of thousands of players on launch day, a then-unprecedented figure. The game introduced the Arbiter, elevated the Covenant narrative, and ended on a notorious cliffhanger. Halo 3 (2007) became one of the fastest-selling entertainment products in history at the time, generating over $170M on its opening day. It concluded the trilogy with the Ark and the Gravemind while embedding Forerunner lore through in-game terminals.
📅 2009–2010 — Bungie's Farewell
ODST, Reach, and the End of an Era
Halo 3: ODST (2009) was a tonal departure — noir, grounded, human. Critically acclaimed and commercially successful despite being a shorter standalone release. Halo: Reach (2010) was Bungie's final Halo game. A prequel depicting the fall of the planet Reach, it featured a cast of original characters (Noble Team) and ended with a direct handoff to CE's opening. Bungie spun off from Microsoft and retained studio independence while 343 Industries was established to carry the franchise forward.
📅 2012–2015 — The 343 Transition
Halo 4, the Forerunner Retcon, and a New Foundation
343 Industries' first major act was the Forerunner Saga novels (Greg Bear, 2011–2013), which formally retconned the Forerunners from humanity's ancestors to a separate alien species. Halo 4 (2012) introduced the Didact, deepened Cortana's rampancy arc, and proved 343 could make a technically excellent Halo campaign. Halo 5: Guardians (2015) was more divisive — a split campaign between Chief and Fireteam Osiris that many felt underserved its premise, though its multiplayer (Warzone, Arena) was widely praised.
📅 2021 — The Revival
Halo Infinite and the Free-to-Play Reset
After a troubled development and a delayed launch, Halo Infinite (2021) released to strong critical reception for its campaign — an open-world Zeta Halo adventure with the Banished as antagonists, the grappleshot as a transformative traversal tool, and a more grounded emotional story. The multiplayer launched as free-to-play, the first in franchise history. Post-launch support has been gradual but sustained.
📅 2022–2024 — Present
The Paramount+ Series and an Uncertain Future
The Paramount+ series (2022–2023) brought Halo to live-action with the deliberate "Silver Timeline" framing — a parallel canon that let the show make different creative choices without contradicting the games. 343 Industries was restructured in 2023 following layoffs, with leadership changes and the franchise's future direction under active re-evaluation. The MCC remains actively played on PC with robust mod support. Community enthusiasm remains high, particularly in the Bungie-era modding scene.

🗺️ Canon Map
What's canon? What's not?

Halo has a two-tier canon situation. The main game timeline (CE through Infinite) is unambiguous canon. The Paramount+ series operates under the "Silver Timeline" — a deliberately separate parallel canon. The Forerunner Saga novels are 343-era canon but retroactively conflict with Bungie-era lore seeding.

🎲 Notable Facts & Trivia
  • Halo CE was announced at Macworld 1999 by Steve Jobs himself, as a game coming to Mac — two years before it launched as an Xbox exclusive following Microsoft's Bungie acquisition.
  • Martin O'Donnell's original Halo theme was composed in a single afternoon and used placeholder Gregorian chant that was meant to be replaced — the team loved it so much it stayed in the game unchanged.
  • The Halo 3 campaign terminals, written by Bungie, explicitly hint that "they" (the Forerunners) and humans are the same species — a thread 343 chose not to follow in the retcon, leading to the two-era creative split.
  • Joseph Staten, the writer of Halo 2 and 3, left Bungie, worked on Destiny, and then returned to 343 Industries specifically to lead the Halo Infinite campaign narrative — a full-circle moment for the franchise.
  • Halo: Reach was produced without knowledge of 343's future Forerunner plans — Bungie had signed off from the franchise entirely by the time Greg Bear began writing Cryptum.
