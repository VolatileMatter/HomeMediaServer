Final Fantasy

// NES → PS5 · PC · Mobile · Anime · Films · Manga · Novels · Audio Dramas //

Created by Hironobu Sakaguchi in 1987 as a last-ditch effort to save Square from bankruptcy, Final Fantasy grew into one of the most influential RPG franchises in history. Spanning 16 mainline numbered entries, countless spin-offs, two MMOs, five films, multiple anime series, a live-action drama, and dozens of novels and manga, each main installment tells a standalone story — yet all share recurring motifs: crystals, chocobos, moogles, summons, and a character named Cid. Use the Library tab for all games in release order. TV & Anime covers anime OVAs and live action. Books & Manga covers novels, novellas, and manga adaptations.

📚 Ultimania & Art Books

Ultimania books are Square Enix's official companion guides — part strategy guide, part art book, part lore bible. Every mainline FF has one; FFXIV has multiple per expansion. They are published in Japanese; some have official English translations; many have been extensively fan-translated. Notable for containing developer interviews, detailed world-building notes, and canonical clarifications not found in the games themselves.

English-available Ultimania volumes: FFVII Remake Material Ultimania, FFVII Remake Ultimania (partial fan translation), FFXV Ultimania (fan translation). Physical copies on Amazon JP or secondhand import sites.

🗂️ Franchise Timeline
1985–1987 — ORIGIN
One Last Gamble
Square was facing bankruptcy. Designer Hironobu Sakaguchi proposed an RPG he called his "final" project — if it failed, he would leave the industry. Final Fantasy released December 18, 1987 on the NES in Japan. It sold 520,000 copies, saving the company.
1988–1994 — NES / SNES ERA
Building the Blueprint
Six mainline entries in eight years, each reinventing the formula: FFII introduced action-based leveling; FFIII the job system; FFIV the Active Time Battle system; FFV refined jobs further; FFVI introduced story and characters of unprecedented emotional depth for the medium. Yoshinori Kitase, Nobuo Uematsu, and Yoshitaka Amano defined the franchise's creative DNA.
1995–2000 — THE PS1 BREAKTHROUGH
3D, CD-ROMs, and World Domination
FFVII (1997) became the defining moment for JRPGs in the West. Its cinematic presentation, 3D graphics, and emotional narrative made it a cultural landmark. FFVIII followed with photorealistic character proportions; FFIX (2000) deliberately returned to the series' classic fantasy roots. Final Fantasy Tactics (1997) established Ivalice as a beloved sub-universe.
2001–2006 — PS2 ERA
Voice Acting, Online Play, and Ambition
FFX (2001) introduced full voice acting and the Sphere Grid. FFXI (2002) launched Square into the MMO market — initially skeptically received, now beloved by its community after 20+ years. FFX-2 became the first direct sequel. FFXII (2006) introduced the Gambit system and drew from Yasumi Matsuno's Ivalice aesthetic. Meanwhile, Square merged with Enix in 2003 to form Square Enix, partly to recover from the catastrophic failure of the Spirits Within CGI film.
2007–2013 — THE HD STRUGGLE
Controversy, Crisis, and Rebirth
FFXIII (2009) was commercially successful but critically polarizing — its linear design and lack of exploration generated years of debate. The FFXIII trilogy concluded with Lightning Returns (2013). More dramatically, FFXIV launched in 2010 to catastrophic failure. Producer Naoki Yoshida was brought in to rebuild it from the ground up; the rebuilt A Realm Reborn launched in 2013 to enormous acclaim, eventually becoming one of the most-played MMOs in the world.
2014–2019 — DEVELOPMENT TURMOIL AND FFXV
Ten Years and a Road Trip
FFXV spent 10 years in development — originally announced as Final Fantasy Versus XIII in 2006. When it finally released in 2016, it was accompanied by the Kingsglaive film and Brotherhood anime, an unprecedented multimedia launch. Positive reception, but four planned DLC episodes were canceled mid-production when director Hajime Tabata departed Square Enix. Their stories were novelized in The Dawn of the Future (2019).
2020–PRESENT — THE REMAKE ERA
Revisiting the Past, Building New Futures
Final Fantasy VII Remake (2020) reimagined Midgar as a full game with a surprising meta-narrative twist. FFXVI (2023) took the series in a darker, more action-focused direction under producer Naoki Yoshida. Final Fantasy VII Rebirth (2024) expanded the Remake trilogy's open world and emotional scope. FFXIV continued to release major expansions (Endwalker 2021, Dawntrail 2024). A third and final part of the FFVII Remake trilogy is in development.
🗺️ Canon Map
What's Connected? What's Not?

Unlike most franchises, Final Fantasy mainline entries are almost entirely self-contained universes. Connections between games are thematic, not narrative.

🎲 Notable Facts & Trivia
  • The name "Final Fantasy" was chosen because it was meant to be Sakaguchi's last project. It wasn't.
  • FFVII took four times longer to develop than any previous FF game, and used prerendered backgrounds because the team couldn't render real-time 3D environments at acceptable quality.
  • Nobuo Uematsu composed all music for FF I–IX with no formal music training — he taught himself piano as an adult.
  • The original FFXIV launch was so bad that Square Enix issued a public apology. The rebuilt version, ARR, has since become one of the most beloved MMOs ever made.
  • FFXV was in development for over 10 years under two different titles (VS XIII → XV) and two different directors before release.
  • The entire FF franchise has sold over 180 million units — placing it among the top 10 highest-selling video game series of all time.
  • FFVII Remake's meta-narrative twist — where characters seem aware they are in a remake — is one of the most discussed and divisive creative decisions in recent JRPG history.
  • The FFXIV community raised over $500,000 USD for charity during Naoki Yoshida's 2021 Fan Festival, primarily through in-game item auctions organized by players.
  • Every mainline FF game has a character named "Cid" — though they are always different characters with no story connection. Only recurring thematic element guaranteed in numbered entries.
