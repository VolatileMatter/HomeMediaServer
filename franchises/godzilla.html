Godzilla

// Showa · Heisei · Millennium · MonsterVerse · Shin · Minus One · Anime · Comics · Games //

The King of the Monsters. Toho's nuclear kaiju franchise began in 1954 as Ishirō Honda's allegory for atomic devastation and grew into the longest-running film series in cinema history — 38 Japanese films and counting. The wider Tohoverse spans Mothra, Rodan, King Ghidorah, Mechagodzilla, Gamera (adjacent), and a vast ecosystem of anime, manga, IDW comics, games, and the Legendary MonsterVerse. Use the Movies tab to navigate all eras with context. The Library tab covers all games, anime, and print. The History tab traces the full 70-year arc from Hiroshima allegory to Oscar-winning spectacle.

🛒 Godzilla Merchandise & Collectibles
Bandai — Vinyl Figures (1983–present)

Bandai's "Godzilla Island" vinyl kaiju figures are the flagship Godzilla collectibles. The Movie Monster Series (MMS) line at ~6" scale has covered virtually every kaiju design across all eras. Premium lines include Bandai Cinema Masterpiece and S.H. MonsterArts for detailed poseable figures. Prices range from ¥1,500 (standard vinyl) to ¥25,000+ (S.H. MonsterArts). A Shin Godzilla S.H. MonsterArts figure remains one of the most sought-after modern releases.

NECA — US Market Figures (2013–present)

NECA's highly detailed 6" and 12" Godzilla figures cover Showa, Heisei, Millennium, and MonsterVerse designs at accessible prices. Their 2014 and KOTM MonsterVerse figures are among the best-value Godzilla collectibles on the US market. Available at specialty toy stores and online.

Jakks Pacific — Giant Figures (2014–present)

24" and 11" budget-accessible Godzilla figures. Limited articulation but excellent value for display scale. MonsterVerse designs. Widely available at Target, Walmart, Amazon.

Trading Cards & TCG

No major TCG exists for Godzilla. Trading cards were produced by Bandai in Japan during the Heisei era. The MonsterVerse has produced promotional Topps cards. Godzilla vs. Kong tie-in card packs were released in 2021.

📖 Franchise History
1954 — Origin: Hiroshima's Monster

Godzilla (Gojira) was created by Tomoyuki Tanaka, directed by Ishirō Honda, with special effects by Eiji Tsuburaya. The film was produced just nine years after Hiroshima and Nagasaki, and six months after the Lucky Dragon No. 5 incident — a Japanese fishing boat irradiated by the Castle Bravo hydrogen bomb test. The film was a direct allegory for nuclear trauma and the impotence of humanity against the forces it had unleashed. Honda described Godzilla as "a god of destruction" and intended no sequel.

1955–1975 — Showa Era: From Horror to Hero

Commercial success of Godzilla Raids Again led to a franchise. As the Cold War shifted and Japan's postwar recovery accelerated, Godzilla's allegory faded and he became a pop culture hero — protecting Japan from alien threats, battling Mothra and Ghidorah, and gaining a son (Minilla). By the late Showa era, the films were primarily aimed at children. Eiji Tsuburaya's suitmation technique — a man in a suit on miniature sets — became the franchise's defining aesthetic. The Showa era ended with Terror of Mechagodzilla (1975) as declining box office prompted Toho to shelve the franchise.

1984–1995 — Heisei Era: Dark Revival

Toho revived the franchise with The Return of Godzilla (1984), ignoring all Showa sequels except the 1954 original. The Heisei series ran a single continuous continuity across seven films, ending with the iconic Godzilla vs. Destoroyah (1995). The era restored Godzilla as a genuine threat, introduced complex serialised storytelling, and produced some of the franchise's most beloved entries — particularly Godzilla vs. Biollante (1989). The death of Godzilla in Destoroyah remains one of the most emotionally impactful moments in the series.

1998 — TriStar American Godzilla: The Low Point

Roland Emmerich and Dean Devlin's American Godzilla was a commercial success but a critical and fan disaster. Toho's president stated the creature "didn't look like Godzilla at all." Toho officially renamed the creature "Zilla" (stripped of the "God" prefix) and later had the Toho Godzilla destroy it in under 14 seconds in Final Wars (2004). The animated sequel, Godzilla: The Series (1998), was far superior to the film and remains fondly regarded.

1999–2004 — Millennium Era: Anthology Reboots

Toho's response to the 1998 American film was the Millennium series — six films, each a standalone reboot with no continuity between them (except the Kiryu duology). The era's standout, GMK: Giant Monsters All-Out Attack (2001), directed by Shusuke Kaneko (who made the Heisei Gamera trilogy), remains one of the greatest kaiju films. The era ended with the 50th anniversary spectacle Final Wars (2004) and another hiatus.

2014–present — MonsterVerse & Toho's New Era

Legendary Pictures acquired the Western Godzilla license and produced a Marvel-style "MonsterVerse" shared universe beginning with Godzilla (2014). In parallel, Toho launched a new era of high-profile standalone films: Shin Godzilla (2016, co-directed by Hideaki Anno) won seven Japan Academy Prizes; Godzilla Minus One (2023, directed by Takashi Yamazaki) won the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects — the first Japanese film to do so — on a reported budget of just $15 million. As of 2025, both the MonsterVerse (ongoing via Monarch: Legacy of Monsters S2) and Toho's new standalone productions remain active.

Godzilla's Cultural Legacy

Godzilla is the most recognisable film monster in history, appearing in 38+ films across 70 years. The franchise directly influenced: kaiju cinema (Gamera, Ultraman, Pacific Rim), American blockbuster spectacle (Jurassic Park, Cloverfield, Interstellar), and Japanese tokusatsu television. Godzilla was awarded a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame in 2004. In 2015, the Japanese government officially appointed Godzilla a "Tourism Ambassador" for the Shinjuku district of Tokyo. The character's endurance across seven decades — and continued reinvention as allegory for contemporary fears (nuclear anxiety, bureaucratic inertia in Shin Godzilla; WWII survival guilt in Minus One) — is without parallel in genre cinema.
