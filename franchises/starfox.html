A brief history of the Star Fox merchandise program.
📅 The N64 Golden Window (1997–2002)

Star Fox 64's massive commercial success generated the franchise's peak licensing activity. Bandai produced model kits for the Japanese market (Arwing, Wolfen), and Nintendo Power distributed promotional figures as subscription rewards. Quality varied widely — the Bandai kits remain genuinely excellent; the Western toy output was cheap and sparse. This window represented the only time Star Fox merchandise was treated as a priority product by licensees.

📅 The Barren Middle Years (2002–2014)

Adventures, Assault, and Command generated almost no licensed merchandise outside of strategy guides and occasional Nintendo-catalog items (keychains, plush distributed at Nintendo World events). Rare's departure and the franchise's declining sales profile made Star Fox a low-priority license. Toy Biz produced a small Adventures-era figure line at poor quality. The Wii era saw zero Star Fox titles and zero Star Fox merchandise.

📅 The Amiibo & Premium Statue Era (2014–present)

The Amiibo platform gave Star Fox its first quality mainstream collectibles — Fox in the original Smash wave (2014), followed by the full four-character Zero 4-pack in 2016. First4Figures entered the franchise with the Fox on Arwing statue in 2017, which remains the most prestigious Star Fox collectible ever produced. As of 2026, Star Fox merch is primarily Amiibo (secondary market) and First4Figures reorder windows. No major new licensed products have been announced, reflecting the franchise's dormant state post-Zero.

Grail Items & Hard-to-Find Pieces

The Bandai Arwing/Wolfen model kits (Japan, 1997) are the most sought-after vintage pieces — prices have climbed as demand from adult collectors outpaced supply. The Wolf O'Donnell Amiibo from the Zero 4-pack is the scarcest modern piece. The First4Figures Fox on Arwing Exclusive edition (LED) rarely surfaces on secondary market. Avoid bootleg Amiibo — they're common for Wolf and Falco specifically.

📖 A History of Star Fox
Overview

Star Fox is Nintendo's space-rail shooter franchise, created by Shigeru Miyamoto and Takaya Imamura in 1993 as a showcase for Argonaut Software's Super FX chip — a co-processor that enabled polygonal 3D graphics on the SNES. The franchise is built around Fox McCloud, the young leader of a mercenary flight team defending the fictional Lylat System from the mad scientist Andross. Critically acclaimed for its cinematic presentation, memorable voice cast, and branching route design, Star Fox 64 (1997) remains the franchise's defining achievement and one of the most mechanically satisfying games Nintendo has ever produced. The franchise has been largely dormant since 2016.

📅 1991–1993 — The Origin
Argonaut, the Super FX Chip, and the Birth of a Franchise
Argonaut Software, a UK team that had impressed Nintendo by porting a 3D demo to the Game Boy without help, approached Nintendo about co-developing hardware to enable polygonal 3D on the SNES. The result was the Super FX chip — a math co-processor soldered inside the cartridge. Shigeru Miyamoto and designer Takaya Imamura developed the Star Fox concept around the chip's capabilities, drawing on Japanese mythology (the kitsune / fox spirit) for the characters and creating a rail-shooter format that hid the hardware's polygon limitations behind fast forward movement. The game shipped in 1993 and was a critical and commercial success, establishing Nintendo as a company willing to push hardware engineering to achieve a cinematic experience.
📅 1995 — The Sequel That Never Was
Star Fox 2 — Complete, Shelved, Vindicated
Star Fox 2 was completed in late 1995 and shown publicly at trade events. It expanded dramatically on the original with real-time planetary defense, two-player co-op, all-range mode sections, and two new female pilots (Miyu and Fay). Ninety-nine percent finished, it was shelved at the executive level — Miyamoto and Minoru Arakawa concluded that releasing a SNES sequel would undermine hype for the forthcoming Nintendo 64 and its presumably superior Star Fox follow-up. The ROM leaked within years and built a devoted fanbase that played it in emulators for two decades. Nintendo finally released it officially on the SNES Classic Mini in 2017, confirming what fans had always argued: it was a genuinely excellent game that deserved better.
📅 1997 — The Apex
Star Fox 64 and the Gold Standard
Star Fox 64 is universally regarded as the franchise's masterpiece. Built entirely in-house at Nintendo EAD with a new team following Argonaut's departure, it introduced full voice acting (English and Japanese dubs both excellent), a 15-stage branching route system across the Lylat System, the Rumble Pak (its pack-in debut), a robust multiplayer mode, and the most quotable script in Nintendo history ("Do a barrel roll," "Can't let you do that, Star Fox," "We're using our Landmaster"). The European/Australian release as Lylat Wars is the same game under a different name due to trademark issues. It sold over 4 million copies and established the franchise's voice cast and world design that persists to this day.
📅 1997–2000 — The Rare Partnership
Dinosaur Planet and the Identity Crisis
After SF64's success, Nintendo approached Rare — riding high on Donkey Kong Country, GoldenEye, and Banjo-Kazooie — about creating a new Star Fox adventure for the N64. Rare developed Dinosaur Planet as a standalone action-adventure with original characters. When Miyamoto saw a late build at Space World 2000, he noted that the main character resembled Fox McCloud and suggested simply making it a Star Fox game. The decision was made; Rare converted protagonist Sabre into Fox, Krystal was repositioned from co-lead to a character to be rescued, and the game was ported to the GameCube to improve visuals. It shipped as Star Fox Adventures in 2002 — Rare's final Nintendo product before Microsoft acquired them.
📅 2002–2006 — The GameCube / DS Era
Assault, Command, and the Quiet Slide
Star Fox: Assault (2005), co-developed with Namco, returned to space combat and was critically well-received. Its Aparoid invasion storyline and on-foot/Landmaster sections drew comparisons to the GC era's action-game trend, but rail sections showed genuine craft. Star Fox Command (2006) for DS was Q-Games' first franchise entry — an experimental strategy/shooter with stylus controls and nine branching narrative endings, none of them especially satisfying canonically. By this point the franchise was clearly losing directional focus, with each entry taking a different genre approach and none recapturing SF64's impact. The Wii era (2006–2012) saw no Star Fox title — an unprecedented six-year gap.
📅 2011–2016 — Remakes, Revivals, and the Wii U Gamble
Star Fox 64 3D, Zero, and the End of an Era
Star Fox 64 3D (2011) gave the 3DS a strong showcase title and was well-received as a faithful remake. It hinted at renewed franchise interest at Nintendo. Star Fox Zero (2016), co-developed with PlatinumGames on Wii U, was announced as a proper new entry and generated significant enthusiasm. The final product's mandatory dual-screen gyro control scheme — requiring players to aim with the GamePad while watching the TV — divided audiences sharply and is widely credited with suppressing both critical reception and sales. PlatinumGames' own designers reportedly had reservations during development. Zero also shipped alongside Star Fox Guard, a tower-defense spinoff. Both titles underperformed commercially and represented the Wii U's final Star Fox chapter. The accompanying WIT Studio anime short, however, was universally praised.
📅 2016–2026 — The Long Silence
Dormancy and Unresolved Futures
As of 2026, no new Star Fox game has been released or officially announced since Star Fox Zero in 2016. The franchise has maintained a presence through Smash Bros. Ultimate (Fox, Falco, and Wolf as fighters; Krystal as Assist Trophy), the 2017 official Star Fox 2 release, and ongoing Amiibo availability. Miyamoto has stated in interviews that he finds the franchise's future directionally unclear — the original space-rail format feels dated, and Adventure-style spinoffs drew criticism. PlatinumGames expressed interest in developing a follow-up to Zero if Nintendo provided the opportunity. Fan advocacy for a Krystal-playable entry, a proper Star Fox 64 sequel, and an HD remaster of Assault persists. The franchise turns 33 in 2026 with its mainline future unresolved.

🗺️ Canon Map
What's canon? What's not?

Star Fox's canon is notoriously unclear — Nintendo has never published an official timeline. The community-consensus understanding is that the SNES game, SF64, Adventures, and Assault form a single continuous timeline. Star Fox Zero is a soft reboot / reimagining of SF64 and sits in a separate continuity. Command's nine endings are all considered "possible futures" rather than definitive canon. The manga "Farewell, Beloved Falco" is official Nintendo material and generally accepted as bridging SF64 and Adventures.

🎲 Notable Facts & Trivia
  • The "Do a barrel roll" instruction from Peppy in SF64 is technically wrong — the maneuver performed is an aileron roll, not a barrel roll. Nintendo has never corrected this. The phrase became one of the earliest widespread gaming memes.
  • Star Fox 2's cancellation created an unusual situation where a complete, polished Nintendo game circulated as a ROM for 22 years before official release. Nintendo finally bundled it with the SNES Classic in 2017, marking its first official public appearance.
  • The Virtual Boy Star Fox prototype (leaked ROM) predates the Virtual Boy's commercial launch. It demonstrates that Nintendo explored Star Fox as a flagship title for the platform before apparent abandonment. The Virtual Boy sold approximately 770,000 units and was discontinued within one year.
  • Argonaut Software's lead programmer Dylan Cuthbert is Japanese-fluent and lived in Kyoto during the original game's development. He founded Q-Games in Kyoto and has maintained ties to the franchise through SF64 3D and Command.
  • The Dinosaur Planet GigaLeak (2021) is considered one of the most significant game preservation events of the decade — providing direct evidence of the game's original design before the Star Fox retrofit, including Sabre's character arc and Krystal's original protagonist role.
  • Falco Lombardi's Amiibo was among the initial Smash wave Amiibo supply scarcities (2014–2015) — reportedly due to a production misjudgment. Used copies sold for multiples of retail price during the shortage, establishing the Amiibo secondary market as a genuine collector phenomenon.
  • Star Fox has appeared in every Smash Bros. entry since Melee (2001). Fox McCloud is statistically one of the most-played characters in competitive Smash history.
