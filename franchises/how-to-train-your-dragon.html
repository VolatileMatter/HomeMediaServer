HOW TO TRAIN YOUR DRAGON

// Books · Films · TV · Games · Audiobooks · Comics · Live Action //

Cressida Cowell's 2003 children's novel series, adapted by DreamWorks into a landmark animated trilogy (2010–2019) following Viking outcast Hiccup and his Night Fury dragon Toothless. One of animation's finest coming-of-age stories, now entering a live-action era with a 2025 remake. Note: the books and films are parallel universes — loosely inspired but telling very different stories. Use the Library tab to browse all media in release or story order. TV & Anime covers the three series plus shorts. Movies has the full theatrical and live-action run.

A brief history of the HTTYD merchandise program.
📅 The Film Launch Era (2010)

Spin Master acquired the master toy license for the first film. The electronic Toothless figure was the flagship SKU. The 2010 toy line was broadly successful but relied on mass-market retailers — quality was functional rather than premium. Plush, basic action figures, and role-play items (Hiccup helmet, Toothless wings) were the anchor products.

📅 The Trilogy Expansion Era (2014–2019)

Each sequel refreshed the toy line with improved sculpts and more dragon species. The Hidden World wave (2019) had notably better quality than earlier releases, especially the Light Fury figures. The franchise never attracted the volume of high-end collector statues that some DreamWorks properties did — most collector-tier pieces came from third-party statue makers rather than official licensed products.

📅 The Modern Licensed Market (2023–present)

The 2025 live-action film triggered a new retail toy cycle with Spin Master again holding the license. Ravensburger's board game entry represented an upgrade in tabletop quality. The Universal Epic Universe theme park opening (May 2025) at Orlando created new venue-exclusive merchandise. Premium statues and figures remain underserved for a franchise of this size.

📖 A History of How to Train Your Dragon
Overview

How to Train Your Dragon began as a children's novel by British author Cressida Cowell in 2003, inspired by the Inner Hebrides islands where she spent her childhood. DreamWorks Animation acquired the rights and produced a loose film adaptation in 2010 that diverged significantly from the books but became one of the most celebrated animated films of the 2000s. The franchise spans two parallel universes — Cowell's 12-book literary series and DreamWorks' film/TV universe — that share a protagonist name and general Viking-dragon setting but tell entirely different stories.

📅 2003–2009 — The Book Years
Cressida Cowell Builds a World
Cowell published the first HTTYD novel in 2003, drawing on her childhood on a remote Scottish island to build a Viking fantasy world where dragons are trainable but dangerous. Eight books followed through 2009, establishing a dedicated readership. The books are framed as Hiccup's memoirs — "discovered" and "translated" by Cowell from the Old Norse — giving them an eccentric, fourth-wall-aware voice that set them apart from other children's adventure series. By the time DreamWorks acquired the adaptation rights, the series had sold millions of copies across Europe.
📅 2010 — The First Film Redefines Animation
How to Train Your Dragon Changes What Animated Films Can Do
Directed by Chris Sanders and Dean DeBlois, the 2010 film fundamentally reimagined the source material — Toothless became a sleek Night Fury rather than a small talking dragon, and the story became one of mutual disability and trust between an outsider boy and a wild creature. Cinematographer Roger Deakins served as visual consultant, and the result was lighting and aerial choreography unlike anything previously seen in animation. The film earned nearly $500 million worldwide and nominations for both Best Animated Feature and Best Original Score (John Powell). It established Powell's score — particularly "Forbidden Friendship" and "Test Drive" — as some of the most iconic in modern cinema.
📅 2010–2019 — The Animated Golden Age
A Trilogy That Grew Up With Its Audience
What distinguished HTTYD from most animated franchises was its deliberate aging of characters and darkening of tone across the trilogy. Film 2 (2014) introduced genuine loss — a principal character dies — and was significantly more emotionally demanding than its predecessor. The TV series (2012–2018) bridged the films with high-quality content that retained the original voice cast. Film 3 (2019) delivered a genuinely bittersweet ending where Hiccup chooses to release the dragons to protect them, echoing real-world themes of environmental coexistence and letting go. All three films received Best Animated Feature nominations; combined box office exceeded $1.6 billion.
📅 2015 — The Book Series Concludes
Cowell's 12-Book Arc Reaches Its Epic Finale
Cowell concluded the novel series with Book 12: How to Fight a Dragon's Fury in 2015, completing an arc that had grown from a light children's comedy into a full-scale epic fantasy about the extermination of dragons and one boy's role as their last defender. The finale sold over 7 million copies of the series worldwide and demonstrated that the literary universe, while parallel to the films, had its own independent emotional weight. David Tennant's audiobook narrations — available for all 12 books — became celebrated in their own right.
📅 2019–2023 — Spinoffs and the Post-Trilogy Era
Expanding the Universe for Younger Audiences
Following the trilogy's emotional conclusion, DreamWorks expanded the brand into younger demographics with Rescue Riders (preschool, 2019) and The Nine Realms (set 1,300 years in the future, 2021). Neither series connected to the main story with the same depth as the original run, and both were primarily aimed at maintaining franchise awareness rather than continuing the core narrative. School of Dragons, the fan-favorite MMORPG, shut down in June 2023 after a decade of operation.
📅 2025 — Present: The Live Action Era
DreamWorks Enters Live Action — and Wins
The 2025 live-action remake marked DreamWorks Animation's first live-action theatrical film. Director Dean DeBlois returned, Gerard Butler reprised Stoick, and Mason Thames and Nico Parker played the younger leads. The film grossed over $600 million worldwide — more than Film 1's original run — and positioned itself as the start of a new trilogy. Universal Epic Universe theme park in Orlando opened its HTTYD: Isle of Berk land in May 2025. Cressida Cowell returned to the book universe in 2025 with How to Train Your Dragon School, a new spinoff series. A live-action Film 2 remake was officially confirmed for June 2027, with Cate Blanchett returning as Valka.

🗺️ Canon Map
Two Universes, One Franchise Name

The HTTYD franchise has two fully independent canons that do not intersect:

Cowell Book Universe: Books 1–12 + the new School spinoff series. Toothless is a small, toothless, talking Common-or-Garden Dragon. The story is Hiccup's memoirs. David Tennant audiobooks are the recommended format. This universe has its own complete arc and ending.

DreamWorks Film/TV Universe: Films 1–3 + all TV series (Riders/Defenders/Race to the Edge) + Dark Horse Comics (co-written by DeBlois, bridges Films 2–3) + live-action remakes. Toothless is a Night Fury. The School of Dragons MMO was semi-canonical before shutdown. Rescue Riders and The Nine Realms exist within this universe but have minimal connection to the main story.

The two universes share: the protagonist's name and general personality, the Viking setting, the title of Book 1/Film 1, and the name Toothless. They diverge on virtually everything else.
