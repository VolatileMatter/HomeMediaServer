Silent Hill

// PS1 · PS2 · PS3 · Xbox 360 · Wii · GBA · Mobile · PC · EmulatorJS · Jellyfin //

Silent Hill is Konami's psychological survival-horror franchise (1999–present), set in a fog-shrouded American town that warps into a nightmare dimension tailored to each visitor's guilt and trauma. The Team Silent era (SH1–SH4) defines the canon; the western-developed era (SH5–Downpour) is divisive; and the franchise has seen a major revival from 2022 onward with Bloober Team's SH2 remake and multiple new projects. Early Era (1999–2007) — Sparse and Import-Heavy

Silent Hill's western merch presence was minimal during the Team Silent era. Most collectibles were Japanese imports — soft vinyl figures, keychains, and strategy guides. The McFarlane Movie Maniacs wave (2001–2004) gave Pyramid Head his first western figure. IDW's comic license (2004–2008) produced the most substantial non-game content of the era.

Film Era (2006–2012) — NECA + Mainstream Exposure

The 2006 Christophe Gans film brought the franchise mainstream visibility. NECA produced film-version Pyramid Head and Nurse figures, though these were based on the cinematic designs rather than the games. The Revelation sequel's poor performance ended the film line. Merch quality and quantity declined as the franchise creatively stagnated in the western developer era.

Premium Era (2014–Present) — Gecco & Revival

Japanese manufacturer Gecco emerged as the definitive Silent Hill collectible maker, producing 1/6-scale game-accurate statues of SH2 and SH3's most iconic figures. The 2022 Konami revival announcement triggered renewed licensing activity — Udon's art book, new apparel, and expanded statue programs. The SH2 Remake (2024) has further accelerated the merch market. The Gecco line remains the benchmark for quality.

Grail Items & What to Avoid

Grail: Gecco 1/6 Pyramid Head (SH2 ver.), Gecco Bubble Head Nurse, Gecco Heather Mason, any Robbie the Rabbit limited edition variant, first-edition BradyGames strategy guides. Avoid: the NECA film-version figures if you want game-accurate designs; bootleg Pyramid Head statues from Wish/AliExpress (poor paint, wrong proportions); the HD Collection on PS3/360.

📖 A History of Silent Hill
Overview

Silent Hill was created by Keiichiro Toyama at Konami's Japan studio in 1999 — a deliberate response to the then-dominant Resident Evil formula. Where RE was action-horror with external monsters, Silent Hill turned inward: the town itself was a projection of its visitors' psychological trauma. The franchise ran through four canonical entries under Team Silent (SH1–SH4, 1999–2004), then passed through multiple western developers, culminating in a near-decade of dormancy bookended by the most famous cancellation in gaming history (Silent Hills, 2015). A major revival began in 2022 with Bloober Team's SH2 Remake and multiple new announcements.

📅 1996–1998 — "Project Hill"
The Fog Begins to Form
Keiichiro Toyama (later director of Forbidden Siren) pitched and began development of what would become Silent Hill at Konami's internal "Team Silent" — a loose collective of young developers across various departments. Inspired by David Lynch's Twin Peaks, Stephen King's novels, and the paintings of Francis Bacon, the team built a psychological horror experience around fog (which also, conveniently, masked the PS1's draw distance limitations). The fog became a design philosophy.
📅 1999 — Silent Hill
Birth of a New Horror Vocabulary
Silent Hill launched for PlayStation in February 1999. It sold modestly but received critical attention for its psychological depth, unsettling monster designs (by Masahiro Ito), and Akira Yamaoka's industrial-ambient soundtrack. The game established the franchise's core metaphor: Silent Hill is not a place but a mirror — it reflects the protagonist's guilt, desire, and trauma back at them in monstrous form. Toyama left Konami after the game shipped to found his own studio (which eventually became SIE Japan Studio).
📅 2001–2004 — The Team Silent Apex
SH2, SH3, and SH4: The Masterwork Trilogy
Under directors Masashi Tsuboyama (SH2) and Masayuki Akutsu (SH3), Team Silent produced the games now considered the gold standard of psychological horror. Silent Hill 2 (2001) introduced James Sunderland and Pyramid Head — both Masahiro Ito creations — and told a story of grief, guilt, and repression that had no precedent in gaming. Silent Hill 3 (2003) was the most technically accomplished entry with the best monster design and art direction. Silent Hill 4: The Room (2004) was an internal pivot that experimented with first-person apartment sequences; it was originally a different game concept (Room 302) retrofitted into the SH universe. Team Silent dissolved after SH4 shipped — the core members dispersed to other Konami divisions or left the company entirely.
📅 2006–2012 — The Western Developer Era
Climax, Double Helix, Vatra: Competent but Missing the Soul
With Team Silent gone, Konami began licensing the franchise to western developers. Climax Studios (UK) made Origins (2007) and Shattered Memories (2009) — the latter is genuinely interesting as a design experiment. Double Helix (US) made Homecoming (2008), which shifted to action-heavy combat and received a mixed reception. Vatra Games (Czech Republic) made Downpour (2012), the most ambitious and technically troubled western entry. The HD Collection (2012, Mass Media Inc.) became infamous for missing fog effects and broken audio — a significant black mark on the franchise's reputation. This era sold the franchise's name without understanding its purpose.
📅 2012–2015 — The Wilderness and P.T.
The Greatest Cancellation in Gaming History
After Downpour's middling performance, the franchise went quiet. Then in August 2014, an anonymous horror game called "P.T." appeared on PSN as a free download. Players eventually discovered it was a Playable Teaser for "Silent Hills" — a collaboration between Hideo Kojima, Guillermo del Toro, and Norman Reedus. P.T. was a looping PS1-sized hallway of escalating psychological horror that became a phenomenon overnight. In 2015, Konami and Kojima Productions parted ways under highly publicized and acrimonious circumstances. Silent Hills was canceled. P.T. was pulled from PSN permanently. The gaming world processed it like a cultural loss.
📅 2015–2022 — The Long Silence
Seven Years of Rumors and Nothing
After the Silent Hills cancellation, the franchise was effectively dead. Konami's public reputation cratered following the Kojima departure and widespread reports about its treatment of employees. Silent Hill existed only in rumors: various developers claiming to have pitched Konami, multiple "revival" rumors every year. Konami became a pachinko and mobile gaming company in the public perception. The franchise's absence made every anniversary a wound.
📅 2022–Present — The Revival
Multiple Fronts, Real Stakes
In October 2022, Konami held a "Silent Hill Transmission" event announcing multiple simultaneous projects: a remake of SH2 by Bloober Team, a new mainline game (Silent Hill f) by NeoBards Entertainment with story by Ryukishi07, a new game (Townfall) by No Code, and the interactive streaming event Ascension. The SH2 Remake launched in October 2024 to significant critical praise — widely considered the best possible outcome for a remake. Bloober Team (previously known for horror games like Layers of Fear and The Medium) executed a faithful modernization. The franchise has genuine momentum for the first time since 2004, with SH f and Townfall still upcoming.
🗺️ Canon Map
What's canon? What's not?

Silent Hill's canon is loosely defined. Each game is largely self-contained with the exception of the SH1/SH3 pair (direct sequel) and the SH2/SH4 pair (thematic/lore connection). The Town itself is the constant; individual games are not tightly continuity-dependent.

🎲 Notable Facts & Trivia
  • Pyramid Head was designed by Masahiro Ito and was never intended as the franchise's mascot — he is specific to James Sunderland's guilt. His appearances in non-SH2 entries (film, Homecoming, SH2 Remake's marketing) have always been controversial with Team Silent alumni.
  • Akira Yamaoka composed every mainline SH soundtrack through Downpour and was rehired for the SH2 Remake. His industrial ambient-rock fusion is so central to the franchise that many fans consider the OST to be the primary horror mechanism — not the visuals.
  • Keiichiro Toyama (SH1 director) created Forbidden Siren after leaving Konami — a spiritual successor to SH's psychological horror approach. He later co-founded Bokeh Game Studio (Slitterhead, 2024).
  • The PS1 version of SH1 used fog not just atmospherically but as a technical necessity — the PS1 could not render more than a short draw distance without it. The design team recognized this limitation as an asset and doubled down on it.
  • P.T. was downloaded approximately 1 million times before it was delisted. PS4 consoles with P.T. still installed have sold on eBay for $1,000–4,000+, with the game itself as the selling point.
  • The SH2 "Leave" ending — in which James and Mary survive — was not intended by Masashi Tsuboyama as the "true" ending. The multiple endings reflect the player's psychological investment in James's guilt or redemption, not a ranked narrative truth.
  • Silent Hill 3 contains a Heather dressing room that can be found to have a message referencing the events of SH2, implying the games exist in the same world but are mostly self-contained tragedies.
  • Konami trademarked "Silent Hills" (plural) in 2015 before officially canceling the project — the trademark was allowed to lapse in 2022, which some fans interpreted as a sign of the revival effort moving forward.
